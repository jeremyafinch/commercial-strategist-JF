Crafting the narratives

Adjacent industries, regulators, acquirers - each needs their own story.

When development cycles last 5-10 years and capital requirements run into the tens of millions, your market story cannot be just a launch asset. It should be a design input - one that shapes what you build, who you hire, and whether money shows up in time.

There are two kinds of marketing (both matter).

Foundational and episodic marketing compared
Foundational marketing Episodic marketing

The first is foundational - it happens early and produces clarity, not campaigns. Why should this company exist? Who is the user, the buyer, and the economic customer? What are you displacing, and what will a rational buyer need to say yes? These are the load-bearing elements of your commercial architecture.

The second is episodic - triggered by specific moments in time: a fundraising or technical milestone, a key hire, a market expansion, a sudden shift in the competitive environment. These moments arrive faster than expected. The companies that handle them well don't scramble because they built the foundation.

You need someone to build the commercial architecture early and then show up ready at the moments that matter.

Hard tech · Materials science

Lux Labs

Lux Labs was commercializing a new transparent display materials - they had real technology, high web traffic, and some early customers, but their go-to-market architecture wasn't built for conversion or scale. I simplified a 30-SKU catalog to two core offerings, rebuilt the pricing model and designed persona-based e-commerce flows around clear customer outcomes rather than dense technical specs. Project delivered 100% ROI within six weeks through improved gross margins and simplified operations. The pricing and packaging structure remained intact through acquisition.
