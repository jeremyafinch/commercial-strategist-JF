Marketing isn't just the last mile.

If you wait until it's ready, you're already too late.

The worst time to figure out your market story is when you urgently need it. But many engineers treat marketing as something to bolt on at the very end, when the product is fully finished. In hard tech, this costs you valuable time and runway.

Extending the runway

A credible investor narrative helps you raise faster, on better terms.

Attracting talent

Mission and story are central to recruiting, hiring, and compensation.

Finding partners

A strategic partner narrative opens early doors that cold outreach can't.

Building social proof

Case studies and proof points can take years to build. Start early.

Entering new verticals

Your second market needs positioning before you've finished the first.

Audience narrative(s)

Adjacent industries, regulators, acquirers - each needs their own story.

When development cycles last 5-10 years and capital requirements run into the tens of millions, your market story cannot be just a launch asset. It should be a design input - one that shapes what you build, who you hire, and whether money shows up in time.

There are two kinds of marketing (both matter).

Foundational and episodic marketing compared
Foundational marketing Episodic marketing

The first is foundational. Why should this company exist? Who is the user, the buyer, and the economic customer? What are you displacing, and what will a rational buyer need to say yes? These are the load-bearing elements of your initial commercial architecture.

The second is episodic - triggered by specific moments: a fundraising or technical milestone, press coverage, a market expansion, a sudden shift in the competitive environment. The startups that handle these well don't scramble reactively.

You need someone to build the early GTM architecture and then show up ready.
