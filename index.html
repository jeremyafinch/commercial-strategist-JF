Marketing isn't just the last mile.

If you wait until it's ready, you're already too late.

The worst time to figure out your market story is when you urgently need it. Yet many engineers treat marketing as something to bolt on at the very end, when the product is finished and the technology is ready. In hard tech, this costs valuable time and runway.

Crafting the narratives

Adjacent industries, regulators, acquirers - each needs their own story.

When development cycles last 5-10 years and capital requirements run into the tens of millions, your market story cannot be just a launch asset. It should be an early design input - one that shapes what you build, who you hire, and whether money shows up in time.

There are two kinds of marketing (both matter).

Foundational and episodic marketing compared
Foundational marketing Episodic marketing

The first is foundational. Why should this company exist? Who is the user, the buyer, and the economic customer? What are you displacing, and what will a rational buyer need to say yes? These are the load-bearing elements of your commercial architecture.

The second is episodic - triggered by specific moments: a fundraising or technical milestone, press coverage, a key hire, a market expansion, a sudden shift in the competitive environment. These moments arrive faster than expected. The startups that handle them well don't scramble reactively.

You need someone to build the commercial architecture early and then show up ready at the moments that matter.

Lux Labs

Lux Labs was commercializing a transparent display film - they had proven MIT technology, significant website traffic, and a few early customers. But a 30-SKU catalog and spec-heavy product pages slowed conversion. I reduced the catalog to two SKUs, rebuilt pricing, and mapped e-commerce flows to customer use cases instead of materials specs and technical data sheets. Project fees paid back in six weeks from improved gross margins. The pricing and packaging structure remained in place through acquisition.
